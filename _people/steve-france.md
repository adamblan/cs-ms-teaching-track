academic-path: I completed a M.S. in Computer Science in 1995 and spent ~25 years at various companies in California including Hewlett-Packard, Raytheon, and General Atomics.  During COVID, my wife and became empty-nesters, and we realized there was an opportunity to try something different (and take on a bit of risk).  I didn't know if any university would hire me with only a M.S. and no formal teaching experience, but I applied to a position at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University in Arizona.  I interviewed virtually, including teaching a sample class (C-programming lecture) online and was successful.  I took a ~65% pay cut from my engineering job to begin teaching; we sold our house, moved to Arizona, and the adventure began.<br><br>The transition was not easy.  I made plenty of mistakes, but was energized by the daily engagement with students, and the ability to utilize multiple facets of my experience and giftedness in the classroom.  There was an enormous amount to learn, which was exciting.  I had very good support from the Center for Teaching & Learning Excellence, and was able learn best practices in pedagogy and experiment with newer concepts like "students as partners" in the classroom.  Starting my academic career at a small, primarily undergraduate institution, in hindsight was very helpful.  It allowed me the space and flexibility to make and learn from mistakes without a lot of the pressures of top-tier schools.<br><br>After 2 years, I cast a rather wide net for my next position, ending up with offers from Carnegie Mellon and Purdue.  I chose the latter for various reasons, including the connection I had with colleagues during the interview process and my desire to continue working with undergraduates.  I am only the 2nd Professor of Engineering Practice to be hired into the Elmore Family of Electrical and Computer Engineering at Purdue University, which is the largest ECE program in the nation.
